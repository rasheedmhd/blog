How to KISS (Keep It Simple Stupid)

These are notes on the Second sub chapter of chapter 1. Chapter One

Be a chef

Someone who can take list of ingredients and cook something amazing without looking at a cook book. The model that helps with that is thinking/arguing from first principles.

First principles

First principles are a group of self-evident assumptions that make up the building blocks of your conclusion/solution. If you can argue from first principles, you can approach unfamiliar situations or form novel ideas to familiar situations. - Thinking from first principles - Thinking from the bottom up, using the most basic components to build up. - Thinking from first principles helps you understand the problem space better, even if you end up agreeing with conventional wisdom

On When Plans Fail

Remember when old Mike said "Everybody has a plan until they get punched in the mouth"? Yeah, I bet you do, essentially your first plan is going to be wrong and may be wildly wrong, you have to plan for that as well, except that this time it is not really a plan it should be taking real time feedback and adjusting your plan appropriately.

This is calling de-risking.

Avoid premature optimization:

Doing too much more than is necessary to test/de-risk an assumption. Why? Because if your assumptions turn out to be wrong, imagine the tremendous amount of time wasted.

Start with an MVP.

The minimum amount of work you need to do to demonstrate an essential part or an idea or product. - Link YC video. Going with an MVP, you are able to evaluate your assumptions quickly and adapt to learn towards what works and what doesn't.

Ockham's Razor

Remember - Ockham's Razor - shave off the complicated and start with the simplest of postulates. It hints that when faced is several assumptions, the simplest is likely to be true. Why address the complicated assumptions when they clearly exists simpler assumptions that helps explain the assumption. - link and quote - By starting with the simple which is likely to be true, we essentially cut off the complicated. Ockham's razor can help with long term decision making like who to date. Rather than have a long list of preference in a partner, you could cast your mind back to KISS, being wrong less, because we care about being wrong less rather than being right? We could go back and look for all the things that made our past relationships to fail and focus on that in dating the next person.

Conjunction Fallacy and Overfitting

Beware of conjunction fallacy and overfitting - in conjunction fallacy the probability of two things occurring in conjunction is less than or equal to one of the events occurring alone. You are biased to think a more specific situation is likely to happen than a generic situation.

Overfitting is using too many assumptions to explain data - inverse of Ockham's razor eg using an overly complicated explanation when a simple one would do. Happens because we are naturally biased towards and and also when we don't take Ockham's razor, unforced errors or fall into conjunctive fallacy.

Ask "how much does my data/situation supports my conclusions versus other conclusions", asking this, and using Ockham's razor to de-risk/evaluate the situation, you avoid conjunction fallacy and overfitting.

All this and this is how you KISS. Keep it Simple Stupid.
